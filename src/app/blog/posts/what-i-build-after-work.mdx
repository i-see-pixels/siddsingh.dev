**Building after work means using side projects to solve real friction, test product instincts, and stay close to curiosity.** I do not treat after-hours projects as a second job. I treat them as a practical lab where I can explore developer tools, AI workflows, and product ideas that are still rough enough to teach me something.

Most of my best ideas do not begin as startup ideas. They begin as friction. Something feels too manual, a workflow takes more steps than it should, or a tool is almost helpful but not quite. When I keep noticing that gap for a few days, I usually want to close it.

I work as a software engineer during the day, but the work I do after hours has a different rhythm. It is more exploratory, more personal, and more direct. There is no roadmap to inherit and no one has already decided what "done" means. I have to decide whether the problem is real, whether the solution is useful, and whether the project deserves another weekend of my time.

## What do I optimize for when I build after work?

The short answer is **usefulness over impressiveness**. I am usually less interested in building the biggest thing in a category and more interested in building the clearest thing in a category.

These are the signals I keep returning to:

- The problem is easy to explain without a long setup.
- The first useful outcome happens quickly.
- The product removes hesitation instead of adding more decisions.
- The scope is small enough to ship before momentum disappears.
- The work teaches me something about product engineering, not just implementation.

That filter shapes almost everything I make outside work. It is why my projects tend to focus on small but meaningful friction points rather than sprawling platforms.

## What kinds of products do I keep returning to?

The products and tools I naturally gravitate toward have a pattern. They reduce uncertainty in the first few minutes.

- [plenz](/work/plenz) helps people improve prompts while they are still thinking.
- [admin-boil CLI](/work/admin-boil-cli) turns dashboard setup from a chore into a starting point.
- [my MCP client for multiple servers](/work/mcp-client-multiserver) makes tool-enabled workflows feel less stitched together.

On the surface those projects live in different categories. Underneath, they are trying to do the same job: remove friction between intention and action.

I care a lot about that space because the first five minutes of using a product shape how people feel about it. If setup is confusing, value is delayed, or the interface asks users to do too much interpretation, momentum disappears quickly. A lot of my work ends up being about fast feedback loops for that reason.

## Why are side projects useful for product engineering?

**Side projects are where I practice product judgment, not just coding.** Shipping small projects has been one of the best ways for me to get better at engineering because side projects force me to think beyond code.

Every serious side project asks the same questions:

- Who is this for?
- What job does it do clearly?
- What should stay rough for now?
- What deserves polish because it affects trust?
- What makes this version different enough to matter?

That mix of engineering and product thinking is the part I enjoy most. I like building systems, but I like it even more when the system solves a problem clearly enough that someone changes their behavior because of it. The code matters and the architecture matters, but if the user still feels confused, the work is not really finished.

## How do I keep scope from getting out of control?

One thing side projects have taught me is that idea quality matters less than scope discipline. A rough but focused idea has a better chance than an ambitious idea with no boundaries.

The question I keep asking is simple:

**What is the smallest version of this that would still feel useful?**

That question saves me from a lot of unnecessary work. It also keeps projects alive long enough to earn the next layer of complexity. Some ideas deserve a landing page and a waitlist. Some deserve a repo and a weekend. Some deserve nothing more than a note in my phone. Learning the difference is part of the craft.

## Why do I keep building after work?

I build after work because it keeps me close to curiosity. It lets me explore AI workflows, browser extensions, developer tools, and product ideas without waiting for perfect conditions. It gives me a place to test instincts quickly and stay practical.

Most importantly, it reminds me that useful software does not have to be huge to matter. Sometimes a product earns attention because it is ambitious. Sometimes it earns attention because it makes one annoying thing feel simple. I am usually more interested in the second category.

That is the kind of work I want to keep doing: small, thoughtful software that helps people move faster, think better, or get unstuck sooner.

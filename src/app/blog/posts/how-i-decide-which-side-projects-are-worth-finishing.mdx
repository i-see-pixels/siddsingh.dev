**I decide which side projects are worth finishing by running them through five questions: problem pull, clarity, scope, learning value, and energy.** That filter helps me separate ideas that deserve real time from ideas that should stay a sketch, an experiment, or a useful lesson.

There is no shortage of ideas. The harder part is deciding which ones deserve real time. I have learned that the danger with side projects is not a lack of motivation. It is scattered motivation. If every interesting idea turns into a repo, progress gets diluted fast and you end up with motion everywhere but momentum nowhere.

## What is the five-question filter I use?

This is the shortest version of the filter I come back to:

| Question | What I am really testing |
| --- | --- |
| Do I care about the problem? | Whether curiosity will survive after the novelty fades |
| Is the value obvious in one sentence? | Whether the idea is clear enough to build around |
| Can I ship a useful narrow version? | Whether the project can become real before it becomes huge |
| Will it teach me something I care about? | Whether the work compounds into better instincts |
| Does it create energy or drain it? | Whether the project is opening up clarity or creating drag |

If an idea fails most of those checks, I usually do not treat it like a real candidate. If it passes most of them, it becomes much easier to justify deeper investment.

## Do I actually care about the problem?

This sounds obvious, but it rules out a lot of ideas quickly. Some projects look attractive because the market is interesting, the category is growing, or the problem seems commercially promising. That still is not enough for me.

If I do not feel a genuine pull toward the problem, I usually will not stay with it once the novelty wears off. The ideas I finish are the ones I keep thinking about even when I am not actively working on them.

## Is the value obvious in one sentence?

If I cannot explain the product simply, I usually do not understand it well enough yet. The strongest ideas get sharper when you compress them.

Examples from my own work:

- [plenz](/work/plenz) improves prompts while you type.
- [admin-boil CLI](/work/admin-boil-cli) installs and configures an admin dashboard quickly.
- [my MCP client for multiple servers](/work/mcp-client-multiserver) lets one client coordinate multiple tool servers from one place.

Those are not full product strategies, but they are strong enough to build around. If the explanation needs too much setup, the product usually needs more thinking before it needs more code.

## Can I ship a useful version without building the whole vision?

This is the question that saves the most time. A lot of ideas sound good only if you imagine the finished version. That is a warning sign. I want ideas that can become useful in a narrow form first.

The MVP I look for usually has four traits:

- it solves one specific job well
- it can be explained quickly
- it creates feedback from real usage
- it does not require months of infrastructure before it becomes testable

When I cannot find that version, the idea may still be interesting, but it is not ready.

## Will the project teach me something I actually care about?

Not every side project needs to become a business. Some projects are worth doing because they improve my taste, sharpen my instincts, or help me explore a technical direction I want to understand better. That is still a good outcome as long as I am honest about it.

I am especially interested in projects that deepen my understanding of:

- AI-assisted workflows
- developer experience
- browser extension constraints
- distribution-first product thinking
- tools that shorten the distance between idea and implementation

If a project fits that path, it can be worth building even before it earns anything.

## Does the project create energy or drain it?

This is the least technical filter and maybe the most important one. Some projects feel heavy almost immediately. Every task expands, every decision feels expensive, and the work starts sounding impressive while the actual process feels dull.

Other projects create energy. As soon as I finish one piece, I want to open the next file. I start seeing distribution angles, the product gets easier to describe, and the next improvement feels obvious. Energy is not a replacement for discipline, but it is a real indicator. When a project keeps creating clarity instead of confusion, it usually deserves more investment.

## What does "finishing" mean for me?

I do not think every project needs a dramatic ending. Sometimes finishing means:

- shipping a usable first version
- publishing the repo
- writing down what worked and what did not
- deciding clearly that I am done

That last one matters more than it seems. A side project does not have to become a long-term commitment to be worthwhile. Sometimes the best outcome is a small shipped artifact and a better instinct for the next one.

I still start too many ideas sometimes. I still underestimate scope. I still occasionally confuse curiosity with commitment. But this filter has helped a lot. It keeps me closer to projects that feel useful, teach me something meaningful, and have a real chance of becoming more than a half-finished experiment.
